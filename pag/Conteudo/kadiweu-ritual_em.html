KADIWÉU

Etogo

The Ship, or Etogo, is the ritual that the Kadiwéu consider the most visible expression of their otherness, because, as they say, it is what best shows that they are "really Indians". Held in 1992, it had not been performed for at least fifty years and was not repeated after that date. The immediate motivation for carrying it out was the need to show white people, their guests, the most expressive ritual of their identity.

The Ship is a long ritual. It makes reference to the Paraguayan War, noted, among other aspects, by the affixing, on each side of the entrance to the ship of boards that were built to be the ritual space, of stylized flags of Brazil and Paraguay. The Ship mimics a warship, just like the ones that the Kadiwéu say they saw, in the past, traveling along the Paraguay River.

The head of the Ship is the character called Maxiotagi, or the "Macho". Maxiotagi is a Xamakôko character (although the actor is Kadiwéu), from the ethnic group that was the main source of captives in the past. His function in the ritual is to dictate the orders that condition the scenes of its development.
